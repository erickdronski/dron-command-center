# Capability Workshop Study Guide
**Ivanti CoE Business Capability Toolkit**
*Workshop facilitation guide — discovery scripts, opening hooks, and conversation starters for each of the 36 capabilities*

---

## How to Use This Guide

For each capability:
- **Opening Script** — brief framing to set context before discussion (2-3 sentences)
- **Discovery Question** — a diagnostic question to surface the customer's current state (often a yes/no that opens a deeper thread)
- **Conversation Starter** — an open-ended question to get the room talking and build momentum

---

## ENTERPRISE SERVICE MANAGEMENT

---

### 1. Incident Management

**Opening Script:**
Incident management is the heartbeat of IT operations — it's how organizations recover when something breaks. Most teams have *some* process here, but there's a massive difference between "we handle tickets" and "we restore services before users even feel it." The question isn't whether you do incident management — it's how fast and how well.

**Discovery Question:**
When a P1 incident hits, do your agents have a single view of the customer — device, history, related incidents — or are they jumping between 4 different tools to piece it together?

**Conversation Starter:**
Walk me through the last time a critical incident hit your environment — how did your team find out, who owned it, and how long did it take to resolve? What would have made that faster?

---

### 2. Problem Management

**Opening Script:**
Problem management is where organizations stop playing whack-a-mole with incidents and start actually fixing the root causes. Incident management handles the fire — problem management rebuilds the fireproofing. Without a dedicated problem process, the same incidents keep coming back, month after month.

**Discovery Question:**
When a major incident is resolved, does your team do a formal postmortem to identify root cause — and is that root cause tracked to a resolution, or does it get closed and forgotten?

**Conversation Starter:**
What are your top three recurring incidents right now? If I asked your problem manager that question, would they give me the same answer — and would they know the status of each one?

---

### 3. Change Management

**Opening Script:**
Failed changes are one of the leading causes of unplanned downtime. Most organizations know this — but their change process is still a bottleneck: too manual, too slow, or too often bypassed because it's easier to just "push the fix." The goal isn't more bureaucracy — it's more confidence and more speed at the same time.

**Discovery Question:**
Do your change requests automatically check for conflicts and dependencies before going to approval, or is that a manual step someone has to remember to do?

**Conversation Starter:**
How many change requests are in your backlog right now, and what's your average time from submission to approval? What's the cost of that delay to the business?

---

### 4. Knowledge Management

**Opening Script:**
Knowledge management is the multiplier that makes every other capability better. When your agents and users can find the right answer in 30 seconds instead of opening a ticket, you get incident deflection, faster resolution, and a better experience — all at once. The challenge isn't writing the knowledge — it's keeping it accurate, findable, and actually used.

**Discovery Question:**
When a new agent joins your team, is there a structured knowledge base they can use to solve common issues independently, or do they mostly learn by asking senior colleagues?

**Conversation Starter:**
If a user has an issue at 10 PM when no one's on support — what happens? Walk me through their options. What percentage of issues could realistically be self-resolved if your knowledge base was better?

---

### 5. Self-Service (Request Management)

**Opening Script:**
Self-service is the most direct lever you have to improve employee experience and reduce ticket volume simultaneously. The best service portals feel like using Amazon — you find what you need, request it, and it shows up. The worst feel like filling out a government form. Most organizations are somewhere in the middle, and the gap between them represents real productivity loss every day.

**Discovery Question:**
Can an employee request a new software license, onboard a contractor, or report an issue from their phone — without needing to call the help desk?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's the most common request your help desk gets that a well-designed self-service portal could handle automatically? How many of those come in each month?

---

### 6. Enterprise Onboarding, Transitions & Offboarding

**Opening Script:**
First impressions are permanent. A new hire who spends their first three days waiting for a laptop and access credentials is already forming an opinion of the organization — and it's not a good one. Onboarding, transitions, and offboarding are often treated as HR problems, but the IT execution is what makes or breaks the experience. When it's automated and orchestrated, it's invisible. When it's not, everyone feels it.

**Discovery Question:**
When a new hire joins, is every step — from device provisioning to application access to workspace setup — automatically triggered the moment HR approves the hire, or does someone have to manually kick off each department?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's the biggest pain point your new hires report in their first two weeks? And when an employee leaves, how confident are you that all access is removed within 24 hours?

---

### 7. Service Level Management

**Opening Script:**
SLAs exist in most organizations — but too often they're a document that lives in a drawer rather than a real-time operating agreement. The difference between an SLA as a contract and an SLA as a management tool is visibility. When your team can see in real time which commitments are at risk, they can act before a breach. When they find out after the fact, they can only apologize.

**Discovery Question:**
Are your SLAs visible to the person who submitted the request — so they know when to expect resolution — or is that information only internal?

**Conversation Starter:**
How many SLA breaches did you have last month? What was the most common cause? And how did you find out — proactively, or when a customer complained?

---

### 8. Service Quality & Analytics

**Opening Script:**
Most IT organizations measure what's easy to measure — ticket volume, average handle time, open tickets. The organizations winning on service quality measure what's hard to measure: sentiment, analyst effectiveness, experience consistency across channels. The gap between what you track and what actually drives customer satisfaction is usually where the improvement opportunity lives.

**Discovery Question:**
Beyond ticket volume and handle time, are you measuring customer satisfaction at the individual analyst level — so you know if specific agents need coaching or support?

**Conversation Starter:**
When was the last time a trend in your service quality data surprised you and led to a decision? What changed as a result?

---

### 9. Analyst Workspace

**Opening Script:**
An analyst's workspace is either a productivity engine or a context-switching nightmare. When an agent has to open six different tabs to see a user's device, open tickets, contact history, and relevant knowledge — every interaction is slower than it needs to be. A unified workspace isn't a nice-to-have; it's a direct KPI driver for first call resolution and handle time.

**Discovery Question:**
When an analyst picks up an incident, do they have the user's device status, recent ticket history, and relevant knowledge articles all on the same screen — or do they have to go find each of those separately?

**Conversation Starter:**
If you asked your analysts what slows them down most, what would they say? How much of that is technology, and how much is process?

---

### 10. Automation & Predictive Intelligence

**Opening Script:**
Automation is no longer about replacing people — it's about multiplying them. The best IT teams aren't the biggest teams; they're the teams whose systems handle the routine so people can handle the complex. And predictive intelligence takes it further: instead of reacting to problems, you're resolving them before users know they exist. That's a fundamentally different operating model.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have automated workflows handling your most common, repetitive tasks — like password resets, access requests, or common software installs — or do agents still handle those manually?

**Conversation Starter:**
If you could remove 30% of your ticket volume starting tomorrow, which categories would you want to eliminate? What's stopping you from automating those right now?

---

### 11. Configuration Item (CI) Inventory

**Opening Script:**
You can't manage what you can't see. CI Inventory is the foundation of almost every other IT management capability — incident routing, change impact analysis, security compliance, asset optimization. Organizations that have an accurate, real-time CI inventory make better decisions across the board. Those that don't are constantly operating on incomplete information.

**Discovery Question:**
If I asked you right now how many devices are connected to your network across all office locations — including cloud, virtual, and IoT — would the number you give me match what's actually there?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's the last time your CI inventory created a problem — because something wasn't in it, or was wrong? How did you find out?

---

### 12. Field Worker & Agent Mobility

**Opening Script:**
The anywhere-workforce isn't coming — it's here. Field workers, remote agents, and distributed teams need to do their jobs without being chained to the office network or a desktop. The organizations that get this right see field productivity that rivals office productivity. The ones that don't see workarounds, shadow processes, and frustrated employees who can't do their jobs on the road.

**Discovery Question:**
Can your field technicians complete a service request, update a ticket, and scan assets from their phone while at a customer site — or do they batch-process everything when they get back to the office?

**Conversation Starter:**
What tasks are your field workers currently doing manually or on paper because the tools don't work the way they need them to? What's the impact of that on resolution speed and data accuracy?

## ENDPOINT MANAGEMENT & SECURITY

---

### 13. Unified Endpoint Management (UEM)

**Opening Script:**
The average enterprise manages dozens of device types across multiple operating systems, owned by the company, leased, or brought by employees. Managing all of that from separate consoles isn't just inefficient — it creates security gaps and visibility blind spots. UEM brings everything into one place: see it, manage it, secure it, from a single interface.

**Discovery Question:**
Today, how many separate tools or consoles does your team use to manage and secure endpoints — Windows, Mac, mobile, cloud, IoT — combined?

**Conversation Starter:**
Where are the biggest gaps in your current endpoint visibility? What types of devices or locations are you least confident about right now?

---

### 14. Endpoint Security

**Opening Script:**
Endpoints are the front door of most cyberattacks. Every device that connects to your network is a potential entry point — and the question isn't if an attacker will try the door, but whether the door is locked. Modern endpoint security isn't just antivirus; it's behavioral monitoring, access control, and continuous compliance verification running in the background.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have continuous visibility into the security posture of every device on your network — regardless of type, OS, or location — or only the ones that are corporate-managed and office-based?

**Conversation Starter:**
If a device on your network got compromised right now — not obviously, just running something it shouldn't — how long would it take you to know? How long before it caused real damage?

---

### 15. Zero Trust Network Access (ZTNA)

**Opening Script:**
Trust but verify is out. Never trust, always verify is in. Zero Trust isn't a product — it's a philosophy: don't assume that because something is on your network, it's safe. Grant access based on identity, device health, and context — not network location. For organizations with hybrid workers, contractors, and multi-cloud environments, this is the only model that actually works.

**Discovery Question:**
When a user connects remotely — whether from home, a coffee shop, or a hotel — do you verify both their identity AND the health of their device before granting access to your applications?

**Conversation Starter:**
What would the impact be to your organization if a contractor's laptop was compromised and used to move laterally through your network? What's currently stopping that scenario from happening?

---

### 16. Physical Asset Inventory

**Opening Script:**
Physical asset management sounds operational, but it's actually a financial story. Organizations that don't have accurate physical inventory end up with emergency purchases, lost equipment, excess stock in the wrong location, and audit failures. The flip side: organizations that do it well reduce costs, accelerate onboarding, and have evidence for every audit.

**Discovery Question:**
If a new hire starts Monday and needs a laptop in a specific office location, can you guarantee that equipment is there, ready, and assigned — or is that a scramble that sometimes fails?

**Conversation Starter:**
Walk me through your most recent audit of physical assets. Where did reality differ from the records — and how did you find out?

## IT ASSET MANAGEMENT & USER EXPERIENCE

---

### 17. IT Asset Management (ITAM)

**Opening Script:**
Every IT asset is a financial liability and a security risk until it's being actively managed. ITAM is how organizations take control of their full asset lifecycle — from purchase to disposal — to reduce cost, reduce risk, and make better decisions. Organizations that do ITAM well often discover they're paying for assets they don't have, or have assets they're not paying for.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you know, right now, the total count of all IT assets your organization owns, where each one is, and who it's assigned to — including assets that are in storage or being repaired?

**Conversation Starter:**
When was the last time you found a significant discrepancy between what you thought you had and what you actually had? What was the cost of that gap?

---

### 18. Software Procurement

**Opening Script:**
Software licensing is one of the most mismanaged cost centers in IT. Organizations routinely overspend on licenses they don't use, or under-license and face audit exposure. The goal of software procurement isn't just buying — it's maintaining an accurate, governed inventory of entitlements that aligns with actual usage at all times.

**Discovery Question:**
If a software vendor audited you tomorrow, would you be able to prove compliance for every application your organization uses — or would there be surprises?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's your current process when a new employee needs software access? From request to active license, how long does that take — and what are the manual steps in between?

---

### 19. Digital User Experience Management (DEX)

**Opening Script:**
DEX is the new employee experience metric. Organizations that only measure IT service through ticket volume and MTTR are measuring the symptom, not the cause. DEX measures the actual productivity of employees on their devices — application performance, login friction, crash rates, digital frustration — and connects it to business outcomes. The best IT teams catch experience degradation before users file tickets.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you currently have a way to measure how employees feel about their technology experience — beyond post-incident surveys — in real time and across all devices?

**Conversation Starter:**
If you could see a productivity health score for every device in your organization right now, what do you think you'd find? Where do you expect the biggest gaps?

---

### 20. Proactive & Remote Support

**Opening Script:**
The traditional IT support model is reactive: wait for a user to report a problem, then fix it. Proactive support inverts that model: detect and resolve before the user even knows something is wrong. Combined with remote access capability, you get a support model that's faster, cheaper, and less disruptive — and one that can handle a fully distributed workforce without sending a technician on-site.

**Discovery Question:**
Today, when an issue is detected on an endpoint — say, low disk space, a failing update, or a security policy drift — does your team know about it before the user does, or after?

**Conversation Starter:**
What percentage of your current support tickets are issues that, if you'd had monitoring in place, you could have resolved before the user ever noticed? What would that be worth in productivity terms?

---

### 21. Dependency & Service Mapping

**Opening Script:**
Most organizations manage their IT infrastructure as individual components — but in reality, everything is connected. A single server going down can take out applications, services, and users in ways that are completely invisible unless you have a map. Dependency and service mapping gives you that map — and it changes how you respond to incidents, how you assess change impact, and how you communicate risk to leadership.

**Discovery Question:**
When a server or application fails, do you have a real-time map that tells you immediately which other services and users are affected — or is that impact assessment something your team has to figure out manually each time?

**Conversation Starter:**
Think about your most critical business services. If one of them went down right now, how confident are you in your team's ability to identify the root cause and the full blast radius within 15 minutes?

---

### 22. Bring Your Own Device (BYOD) Management

**Opening Script:**
BYOD is no longer optional — most organizations have employees using personal devices for work, whether officially or not. The risk isn't the device; it's what the device accesses. Effective BYOD management lets organizations embrace the flexibility of personal devices without compromising security — by separating and controlling corporate data without touching personal data.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have a formal BYOD policy that specifies exactly what data employees can access on personal devices — and the technical controls to enforce that boundary?

**Conversation Starter:**
If an employee's personal phone was lost or stolen right now — with corporate email and files on it — what's your process? How long would it take to secure your data?

---

### 23. Mobile & Field Device Security

**Opening Script:**
Mobile devices are the most underprotected entry point in most enterprise environments. They travel everywhere, connect to unknown networks, run third-party apps, and are frequently lost or stolen. Yet they're also how a growing portion of work gets done. Getting mobile security right means your employees can be productive from anywhere without opening the organization to risk.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have visibility into and control over every mobile device that accesses corporate email or applications — including the ability to remotely wipe a compromised or lost device?

**Conversation Starter:**
How many mobile devices in your organization would you say are "managed" versus just provisioned? What's your level of confidence in the security posture of the ones you're not actively managing?

---

### 24. IoT / IIoT Access Security

**Opening Script:**
IoT devices are the fastest-growing attack surface in enterprise environments — and the least managed. Card readers, cameras, HVAC controls, smart printers, conference room systems — most of them connect to the same network as your servers and user devices, and most of them have never been patched. Securing IoT isn't just a security initiative; it's an operational necessity.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have a complete, accurate inventory of every IoT device on your network — including devices that weren't deployed by IT?

**Conversation Starter:**
When was the last time you audited your IoT environment? What did you find that surprised you, and what's still unresolved?

## EXPOSURE MANAGEMENT

---

### 25. Risk-Based Vulnerability Management (RBVM)

**Opening Script:**
Traditional vulnerability management treats every CVE the same: scan, list, patch in order. Risk-based vulnerability management asks a smarter question: which vulnerabilities, on which assets, present the greatest actual risk to our organization right now? That shift in framing — from volume to risk — changes everything about how your team prioritizes and operates.

**Discovery Question:**
When your vulnerability scan produces results, is your team prioritizing remediation based on actual risk to your environment — taking into account asset criticality and active threat intelligence — or primarily based on CVSS score?

**Conversation Starter:**
What percentage of your open vulnerabilities do you realistically expect to remediate in the next 30 days? What's blocking the others — and how do you decide which ones to let age?

---

### 26. Attack Surface Management

**Opening Script:**
Your attack surface isn't just what you know about — it's everything that's internet-facing, including assets you've forgotten, subsidiaries you've acquired, and shadow IT your teams deployed without telling you. Attack Surface Management is about continuously discovering what an attacker sees when they look at your organization from the outside. Because they're looking, whether you are or not.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have continuous monitoring of your internet-facing assets — including APIs, cloud resources, and third-party subsidiaries — for exposures and new unknowns?

**Conversation Starter:**
If an attacker searched for entry points into your organization right now, what do you think they'd find? How confident are you that you know everything that's exposed?

---

### 27. Patch & Deployment Controls

**Opening Script:**
Unpatched systems are still the leading cause of breaches — not because organizations don't know patching matters, but because patching at scale, across diverse device types, without disrupting operations, is genuinely hard. The organizations that crack this do it through automation, scheduling discipline, and a clear policy for what gets patched, when, and how.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have automated patch deployment cycles running on a defined schedule — including third-party applications and non-Windows devices — or is patching still primarily a manual process?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's your current patch compliance rate? What types of devices or application categories are consistently behind — and what's preventing you from closing those gaps?

---

### 28. Application Security Posture Management

**Opening Script:**
As organizations build more internal applications — and depend on more vendor applications — application security has become a discipline of its own. Application Security Posture Management is about continuously assessing the risk exposure of every application in your portfolio, from code to runtime, and making that information actionable for both developers and security teams.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have automated security scanning integrated into your development lifecycle — so vulnerabilities are caught before applications reach production — or is security assessment primarily a post-deployment activity?

**Conversation Starter:**
Think about your most business-critical application. When was it last assessed for security vulnerabilities? What did that process look like, and how were findings prioritized and tracked?

## FOUNDATIONS

---

### 29. Governance, Risk & Compliance (GRC)

**Opening Script:**
GRC is either a strategic advantage or a compliance burden — and the difference is usually whether it's centralized and automated, or fragmented and manual. Organizations that treat GRC as a living operational practice — rather than an annual audit preparation exercise — have better visibility, faster response to risk, and significantly lower cost of compliance.

**Discovery Question:**
Are your governance, risk, and compliance processes managed in a single system that gives all relevant stakeholders real-time visibility — or are they spread across spreadsheets, SharePoint folders, and individual team processes?

**Conversation Starter:**
When a new regulatory requirement comes in — say, a new data privacy law or a security framework update — what does your process look like to assess your current gap and remediate? How long does that typically take?

---

### 30. Data Management

**Opening Script:**
Bad data is more dangerous than no data. Organizations make decisions every day based on data they trust — but if that data is stale, duplicated, inconsistently formatted, or accessible to the wrong people, those decisions are built on a false foundation. Data management isn't glamorous, but it's the infrastructure that makes everything else reliable.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have a defined data governance framework — with clear ownership, quality standards, and access controls for your critical data sets — or is data quality primarily someone's informal responsibility?

**Conversation Starter:**
When was the last time a data quality issue caused a real problem — a wrong report, a bad decision, a compliance finding? What was the root cause, and is it fixed?

---

### 31. Continual Improvement & Process Optimization

**Opening Script:**
Every organization says it values continuous improvement — but most don't have a structured, measurable process for it. The difference between organizations that improve incrementally and organizations that transform is whether improvement is a cultural habit with defined cadences and executive sponsorship, or a reaction to crises.

**Discovery Question:**
Do you have a recurring, scheduled review process for your highest-volume operations — with a defined methodology like Agile, Lean, or Kaizen — or does process improvement happen primarily in response to problems?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's one operational bottleneck in your IT environment that everyone knows about but that hasn't been resolved? What's the story behind why it's still there?

---

### 32. Business Continuity

**Opening Script:**
Most organizations have a disaster recovery plan. Fewer have actually tested it. Fewer still have a plan that covers not just catastrophic outages but the more common disruptions — ransomware, a key system going offline, a critical vendor failure. Business continuity planning is about making sure your organization knows what to do before the crisis, not during it.

**Discovery Question:**
When was the last time you tested your disaster recovery plan end-to-end — actually failing over, not just reviewing the documentation? What happened?

**Conversation Starter:**
If your primary data center went offline right now, what's your recovery time objective, and do you have confidence you could actually meet it? What are the weakest links in your current plan?

---

### 33. Network Discovery

**Opening Script:**
You can't protect your network if you don't know what's on it. Network discovery is the foundation of visibility — active and passive scanning that maintains a current, accurate picture of every device, connection, and node across your environment. As networks become more dynamic — with cloud, IoT, and hybrid work — the window between "device connected" and "device discovered" is a security and management gap.

**Discovery Question:**
Are your network scans scheduled to run on a regular cadence, with results automatically updating your CMDB — or is your network inventory primarily a snapshot from the last time someone ran a manual scan?

**Conversation Starter:**
What percentage of your network traffic today is from devices you didn't specifically provision? How do you find out when something unexpected connects?

---

### 34. Third Party Connectors, APIs & Integrations

**Opening Script:**
No IT environment operates in isolation. Your ITSM platform, your security tools, your HR system, your monitoring solutions — they all generate data that's more valuable when it flows between them. Third-party integrations are how you turn a collection of tools into a connected system. Done well, they eliminate manual data entry, accelerate workflows, and create the full-picture visibility that drives better decisions.

**Discovery Question:**
When data changes in your core systems — say, an employee is onboarded in HR, or a device is flagged in your security tool — does that automatically trigger the relevant workflows in your other platforms, or does someone have to manually copy that information across?

**Conversation Starter:**
Where are the most manual, error-prone handoffs in your current IT processes? What data is being re-entered or copy-pasted that shouldn't be?

---

### 35. Configuration Management Database (CMDB)

**Opening Script:**
The CMDB is the single source of truth for your IT environment — but only if it's accurate. An outdated or incomplete CMDB isn't just useless; it's actively harmful — it sends teams chasing wrong information during incidents, undermines change impact assessments, and erodes trust in the whole IT management system. A great CMDB is the result of automated discovery, clear governance, and continuous maintenance.

**Discovery Question:**
Is your CMDB populated and maintained primarily through automated discovery — so it updates in real time as your environment changes — or is manual data entry still a significant part of how it gets updated?

**Conversation Starter:**
How much do your teams trust the data in your CMDB right now? When there's a discrepancy between what the CMDB says and what's actually in the environment, what's the process for resolving it?

---

### 36. Reporting

**Opening Script:**
Reporting should answer questions, not create them. Organizations that report well don't just produce data — they produce insight. The difference is usually whether reports are designed around the decisions they need to support, or built around the data that's easy to pull. Self-service reporting is the end state: any stakeholder should be able to get the data they need, on demand, without waiting on IT to build a new report.

**Discovery Question:**
Can your business stakeholders access the reports they need on demand — without submitting a request to IT and waiting — or is report creation still a bottleneck in your organization?

**Conversation Starter:**
What's the most important question your leadership asks that you currently can't answer with data? What would it take to be able to answer it?
